5 Common Social Engineering Techniques and How to Defend Against Them

Posted on March 28, 2023

Social engineering is a growing threat to the security of organizations and individuals. Cybercriminals use manipulation and deception to gain unauthorized access to sensitive information, commit fraud, or infect computer systems with malware. Understanding the most common social engineering techniques and how to defend against them can help protect your business from cyberattacks.

1. Phishing

Phishing is a widespread social engineering technique where attackers send fraudulent emails or messages, imitating reputable organizations, to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information, such as login credentials or credit card information. These emails may contain malicious links or attachments that, when clicked, can install malware or lead to a fake website designed to steal information.

How to Defend Against Phishing:

  • Train employees to recognize phishing emails and avoid clicking on suspicious links or opening attachments from unknown sources.
  • Use email filters and antivirus software to block malicious emails.
  • Implement two-factor

    2. Pretexting

    Pretexting involves creating a fabricated scenario or assuming a false identity to manipulate individuals into divulging sensitive information. Attackers may pose as a coworker, IT support, or law enforcement to gain trust and extract information from their targets.

    How to Defend Against Pretexting:

    • Establish and enforce policies for verifying the identity of individuals requesting sensitive information.
    • Train employees to be cautious about sharing information with unverified sources and to report suspicious requests to the appropriate personnel.
    • Limit the amount of personal and company information available on public platforms, such as social media, to reduce the chances of pretexting attacks.

    3. Baiting

    Baiting is a technique where cybercriminals use enticing offers or incentives to lure victims into revealing sensitive information or downloading malware. This could involve leaving USB drives with malicious files in public places, or offering free software downloads that contain hidden malware.

    How to Defend Against Baiting:

    • Implement strict policies regarding the use of external storage devices and the downloading of software from unknown sources.
    • Train employees to be wary of too-good-to-be-true offers and to report suspicious situations to the IT department or management.
    • Keep antivirus and malware protection software up to date to detect and block potential threats.

    4. Quid Pro Quo

    Quid pro quo attacks involve an attacker offering a service or assistance in exchange for sensitive information or access to a system. For example, they may pretend to be IT support and request login credentials to "fix" a problem that doesn't exist.

    How to Defend Against Quid Pro Quo:

    • Establish protocols for verifying the identity of individuals offering assistance or requesting information in exchange for services.
    • Train employees to question the legitimacy of unsolicited offers and to report any suspicious requests to their supervisor or IT department.
    • Limit the information shared on public platforms and social media to minimize attackers' ability to craft convincing scenarios.

    5. Tailgating

    Tailgating, also known as "piggybacking," occurs when an attacker gains unauthorized access to a restricted area by following closely behind an authorized individual. Once inside, they can steal sensitive information, plant malware, or compromise physical security measures.

    How to Defend Against Tailgating:

    • Implement strict access control measures, such as key cards, biometric scanners, or manned security checkpoints, to ensure no unauthoized access.
    • Ensure employees are trained in control measures and to question suspicious behaviour as part of the company culture.

      Conclusion

      There you have it, 5 of the most common Social Engineering Techniques that an attacker may use to infiltrate your organization. There are more sub categories to many of these methods such as SMS Phishing and Voice Physing.
